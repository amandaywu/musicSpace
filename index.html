How We Visualize Audio Features

We visualize a song based on 9 audio features populated by Spotify based on the characteristics
of the song. Below is a breakdown of each audio feature we’ve used in the visualization above.

Explore a Feature by Comparing 2 Tracks











Danceability

Danceability describes how suitable a track is for dancing based on a combination of musical
elements including tempo, rhythm stability, beat strength, and overall regularity. A value of
0.0 is least danceable and 1.0 is most danceable.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use a jagged line to represent dancability because the edges and sharp points remind us of
pulse and represents movement. The higher the danceability is, the more jagged the line is.

Energy

Energy is a measure from 0.0 to 1.0 and represents a perceptual measure of intensity and activity.
Typically, energetic tracks feel fast, loud, and noisy. For example, death metal has high energy, while
a Bach prelude scores low on the scale.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use transparency of the entire picture to represent energy because we think an energeteic song will
have more presence and therefore has less transparency.

Acousticness

Acoustic music primarily uses instruments that produce sound through acoustic means, as opposed
to electric or electronic means.A confidence measure from 0.0 to 1.0 of whether the track is acoustic.
1.0 represents high confidence the track is acoustic.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We decided to use line to represent the precense of additional track/instrument. A song with acoustic
score close to 1.0 will have less line and vice versa. The total amount of lines to display is 10.
(when acoustic score is from 0.0-0.1)

Instrumentalness

Predicts whether a track contains no vocals. Rap or spoken word tracks are clearly “vocal”.
The closer the instrumentalness value is to 1.0, the greater likelihood the track contains no vocal
content. Values above 0.5 are intended to represent instrumental tracks, but confidence is higher
as the value approaches 1.0.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use the magnitude of the sun/moon to represent instrumentalness. The size of the sun represents
the amount of vocal content. Music with a high instrumentalness score will result in a very small sun/moon.

Liveness

Detects the presence of an audience in the recording. Higher liveness values represent an increased
probability that the track was performed live. A value above 0.8 provides strong likelihood
that the track is live.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use the magnitude of green mountains to represent liveness because mountains convey a sense of
liveliness and energy normally seen from the audience in musical performances. A high liveness score
will results in taller/bigger sets of mountains in the visualization.

Speechiness

Speechiness detects the presence of spoken words in a track. The more exclusively speech-like the
recording, the closer to 1.0 the attribute value. Values between 0.33 and 0.66 describe tracks that
may contain both music and speech, either in sections or layered, including such cases as rap music.
Values below 0.33 most likely represent music and other non-speech-like tracks.


How we choose the visual encoding:

The animated cloud represents the speechiness of a song. Higher speechiness score will generate more cloud,
and if a speechiness score is close to 1, the visualization will be covered by cloud.

Tempo

The overall estimated tempo of a track in beats per minute (BPM). In musical terminology, tempo is
the speed or pace of a given piece and derives directly from the average beat duration.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use the wave to represent tempo because we think the movement in water can represent the speed or pace of a song.
If the tempo number is high, the waves will be more dense to reflect the fast pace.

Valence

A measure from 0.0 to 1.0 describing the musical positiveness conveyed by a track. Tracks with high
valence sound more positive (e.g. happy, cheerful, euphoric), while tracks with low valence sound more
negative (e.g. sad, depressed, angry).


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use the color scheme of the background to represent valence because we think color communicates mood
very well, and valence is a representation of the overall mood of the song. When the song has a high valence
score, it tends to be happier, and we increase the amount of warm tone in the color gradient, and vice versa.

Mode

Mode indicates the modality (major or minor) of a track, the type of scale from which its melodic
content is derived. Major is represented by 1 and minor is 0.


How we choose the visual encoding:

We use a sun/moon to represent mode because of the melodic difference between major and minor
music is very different. Some describes that there is a certain sound-quality to major and
minor. We display the sun when the music is major and the moon when the music is minor.





How We Visualize Lyrics


This 10 x 10 matrix represents 10 words of the sentence "You, you make me, make me, make me wanna cry."


Each row and column of the matrix corresponds to a word in the lyrics.
A grid cell is filled in if its row has the same word as its column.
The color of of the cell is determined by the frequency of words in the song.

How we assign color:
  1. Normalize # of count for each word in the matrix (# count/max count)
  2. Plot the matrix with colormap YlGnBu based on the relative score from 0-1
